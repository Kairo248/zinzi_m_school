About Me

Professional Biography

My name is Zinzi Mboniswa, a dedicated and experienced property management professional with over fourteen years of industry experience. Born and raised in Cape Town, I bring a deep understanding of the local property landscape, supported by both practical expertise and strong academic grounding.

I matriculated in 2006 from Rhodes High School in Cape Town and continued my studies at the Cape Peninsula University of Technology (CPUT). I have since completed my undergraduate qualifications and am currently furthering my academic journey by pursuing a Postgraduate Diploma in Business Administration, reflecting my commitment to continuous professional and lifelong learning (Kolb, 1984).

Over the years, I have built a solid career within the property sector, progressing through key roles that have strengthened my operational, administrative, and leadership capabilities. I have served in positions such as Residential Manager and Assistant Property Manager, where I have been responsible for overseeing property operations, managing tenant relationships, coordinating maintenance, and supporting strategic property decisions. These responsibilities align with the practical application of management functions such as planning, organising, leading, and controlling (Fayol, 1949).

Beyond my professional life, I am a wife and mother of two. These roles have significantly shaped my interpersonal skills, emotional intelligence, and resilience. Balancing career and family responsibilities has enhanced my ability to manage competing priorities effectively, an essential competency in today's dynamic management environment (Goleman, 1998).

Management Experience

  • Residential Manager

In these roles, I have been responsible for managing residential properties, ensuring tenant satisfaction, coordinating service providers, handling conflict resolution, and maintaining compliance with property regulations. These responsibilities reflect the application of both technical and interpersonal managerial skills, as highlighted in Katz's three-skill approach (Katz, 1974).

Through these experiences, I have developed key managerial competencies such as decision-making, communication, and team coordination. I have also learned the importance of adaptability in a fast-paced work environment, which aligns with contingency theory, suggesting that effective management depends on situational factors (Fiedler, 1967).

Leadership Philosophy

My leadership philosophy is grounded in people-centred and ethical leadership, with a strong emphasis on communication, accountability, and empowerment. I believe that effective leadership is not about authority, but about influence, trust, and the ability to inspire others toward a shared goal.

This perspective is strongly aligned with Transformational Leadership Theory, which emphasises motivating and inspiring followers to exceed expectations (Bass & Avolio, 1994). I strive to foster a positive and inclusive work environment where individuals feel valued and empowered.

In addition, I draw on Servant Leadership principles, where the leader prioritises the needs and development of team members (Greenleaf, 1977). By supporting others and leading with empathy, I aim to build strong, cohesive teams.

I also recognise the importance of Situational Leadership, adapting my leadership style based on the needs of the team and the complexity of tasks (Hersey & Blanchard, 1988). This flexibility allows me to respond effectively to different challenges and organisational demands.

Key Insights Gained from the Module

One key insight gained from this module is that effective management is not a one-size-fits-all approach, but requires emotional intelligence, adaptability, and context-sensitive decision-making. Across topics such as transformational leadership, scientific management, sensemaking, managing individuals, managing teams, and managing conflict, I learned that structured systems improve consistency and efficiency, but must be balanced with empathy, communication, and people development.

I also gained a deeper understanding that management effectiveness depends on how theory is interpreted and applied in real situations. Real-world environments are often complex and unpredictable, and successful leadership requires balancing accountability with support, efficiency with well-being, and authority with collaboration.

How Management Theory Will Influence Your Future Managerial Practice

Management theory will influence my future managerial practice by guiding how I design systems, lead people, and respond to operational challenges. I will apply structured processes, performance monitoring, and clear standards to improve service delivery, while also maintaining flexibility to address human needs, workload pressures, and changing circumstances.

In practice, I will use transformational and servant leadership principles to motivate and develop team members, apply situational leadership to adapt to team and task needs, and use collaborative conflict management and sensemaking approaches to improve communication, alignment, and problem-solving. This integrated approach will help me lead in a way that is efficient, ethical, and sustainable.

Critical Reflection 1: Frederick Winslow Taylor's Scientific Management Principles

Introduction

Frederick Winslow Taylor's theory of scientific management remains one of the most influential and debated foundations in management studies. Developed in the early 20th century, it emphasizes efficiency, standardization, and the optimization of work processes (Taylor, 1911). While often critiqued for its mechanistic view of workers, its principles continue to shape modern operational management. Reflecting on this theory has significantly deepened my understanding of efficiency, control, and the balance between productivity and human-centered management.

What interested or inspired you about this topic?

What initially drew my attention to scientific management was its structured and systematic approach to improving organizational efficiency. The idea that work processes could be analyzed scientifically to maximize productivity resonated strongly with my experience in property management, where operational delays such as late maintenance responses or slow processing of tenant applications can negatively affect service delivery.

In particular, Taylor's emphasis on "one best way" of performing tasks highlighted the importance of eliminating inefficiencies. In my role managing 167 apartment units, inefficiencies often emerge in maintenance coordination and tenant communication. The notion that these processes can be standardized and optimized was both inspiring and practically relevant.

What new insights or understanding did you gain?

Through engaging with this theory, I gained a deeper appreciation for the importance of process design and performance measurement. Taylor's principles such as time studies, task specialization, and performance-based incentives demonstrate how structured systems can enhance productivity (Witzel, 2017).

One key insight was the value of standard operating procedures (SOPs). For example, in managing maintenance requests with a 24-hour turnaround time, having clearly defined workflows ensures accountability and consistency. Without structured processes, tasks can easily be overlooked, leading to tenant dissatisfaction.

Another important realization was the role of data-driven decision-making. Taylor's emphasis on measurement aligns with modern management practices such as performance metrics and key performance indicators (KPIs). In leasing and marketing, for instance, tracking enquiry conversion rates and identifying patterns in illegitimate applications can improve screening efficiency.

How did this topic challenge your assumptions or expand your perspective on management?

Despite its strengths, scientific management challenged my assumption that efficiency alone leads to effective management. Taylor's model tends to treat workers as components of a machine, focusing more on productivity than on employee well-being (Braverman, 1974). This raised critical questions about sustainability and employee morale.

In my experience, particularly when dealing with maintenance teams or administrative staff, overly rigid processes can lead to frustration and burnout. For example, enforcing strict turnaround times without considering workload or unforeseen challenges may reduce morale and ultimately impact performance.

This reflection expanded my perspective by highlighting that management is not purely about efficiency - it also requires empathy, adaptability, and an understanding of human behavior. It became clear that while systems are essential, they must be balanced with human-centered leadership.

How will you apply this knowledge in your current or future managerial practice?

In my current role, I can apply Taylor's principles in a balanced and context-sensitive manner. For example:

  • Standardizing processes: Implementing clear procedures for tenant screening, including structured credit and background checks, can reduce the risk of illegitimate applications.
  • Improving efficiency in evictions: The legal eviction process sending reminders, escalating to legal departments, and obtaining court orders can be streamlined through checklists and timelines to ensure compliance and efficiency.
  • Performance tracking: Monitoring maintenance response times and identifying bottlenecks can help improve service delivery.

However, I would apply these principles with flexibility. For instance, while maintaining a 24-hour turnaround target, I would ensure that staff workloads are manageable and that exceptions are handled with discretion. This approach integrates efficiency with employee well-being.

What connections can you make between theory and real-world management practice?

Scientific management is highly visible in real-world property management operations. The eviction case I encountered where a tenant failed to pay rent for over seven months and refused to vacate until a court order was enforced illustrates the importance of structured processes. The step-by-step legal procedure reflects Taylor's emphasis on systematic execution.

Similarly, leasing processes such as listing units, screening tenants, and verifying applications mirror the principles of task specialization and standardization. Each step is designed to maximize efficiency and minimize risk.

However, real-world management also reveals the limitations of Taylor's approach. For example, affluent tenants often demand immediate maintenance responses, disregarding the 24-hour policy. While scientific management would emphasize adherence to the system, effective management requires negotiation, communication, and expectation management.

What tensions exist between theory and real-world management?

A key tension between scientific management and real-world practice lies in the balance between efficiency and human factors. While the theory promotes standardization, real-world scenarios are often unpredictable and require flexibility.

Another tension is between control and autonomy. Taylor's model centralizes decision-making and limits worker discretion, whereas modern management emphasizes empowerment and engagement. In my experience, allowing staff some autonomy in handling tenant issues can lead to more effective and responsive service delivery.

Additionally, there is a tension between short-term productivity and long-term sustainability. Overemphasis on efficiency can lead to burnout, reduced job satisfaction, and high staff turnover - issues that ultimately undermine organizational performance.

Conclusion

Reflecting on Frederick Winslow Taylor's scientific management principles has provided valuable insights into the importance of efficiency, structure, and process optimization. At the same time, it has highlighted the limitations of a purely mechanistic approach to management.

This reflection has reinforced the need for a balanced approach - one that integrates the strengths of scientific management with human-centered leadership. In my managerial practice, I will strive to design efficient systems while remaining responsive to the needs of both employees and tenants. Ultimately, effective management is not just about doing things efficiently, but about doing them in a way that is sustainable, ethical, and responsive to real-world complexities.

Critical Reflection 2: Managing Individuals

Introduction

Managing individuals is a foundational aspect of effective leadership, focusing on understanding employee behavior, motivation, and performance. This topic draws heavily on theories such as Abraham Maslow's hierarchy of needs and Frederick Herzberg's two-factor theory, which emphasize the importance of both intrinsic and extrinsic motivators in shaping employee performance. Reflecting on this topic has deepened my appreciation of the complexity of human behavior in organizational settings.

What interested or inspired you about this topic?

What particularly interested me about managing individuals is the recognition that employees are not homogeneous; they have different motivations, expectations, and responses to management styles. In my role, I have observed how team members respond differently to pressure, deadlines, and tenant demands.

For instance, when managing maintenance staff responsible for a 24-hour turnaround, some employees thrive under structured expectations, while others struggle with the pressure. This diversity in responses highlighted the need for individualized management approaches rather than a one-size-fits-all strategy.

What new insights or understanding did you gain?

Engaging with this topic provided valuable insights into the importance of motivation and emotional intelligence in management. Abraham Maslow (1943) suggests that individuals are motivated by a hierarchy of needs, ranging from basic physiological needs to self-actualization. This framework helped me understand why some employees prioritize job security, while others seek recognition or career growth.

Similarly, Frederick Herzberg (1959) distinguishes between hygiene factors (e.g., salary, working conditions) and motivators (e.g., achievement, recognition). This distinction clarified why simply addressing complaints or providing basic conditions does not necessarily lead to high performance.

How did this topic challenge your assumptions or expand your perspective on management?

This topic challenged my initial assumption that clear instructions and monitoring are sufficient to ensure performance. I realized that even with well-defined processes, employee engagement can vary significantly.

For example, in dealing with leasing and tenant screening, administrative staff sometimes become overwhelmed by the volume of enquiries, particularly illegitimate applications. Initially, I viewed delays as a performance issue. However, this topic helped me recognize that workload, stress, and lack of motivation also play critical roles.

How will you apply this knowledge in your current or future managerial practice?

In practice, managing individuals requires a more personalized and supportive approach. I would:

  • Tailor communication and expectations based on individual strengths and challenges
  • Provide recognition and feedback to enhance motivation
  • Offer support and resources to manage workload effectively

For instance, when handling high volumes of tenant enquiries, I can implement workload distribution strategies and provide clearer guidelines to reduce stress and improve efficiency.

What connections can you make between theory and real-world management practice?

Theories of motivation are highly relevant in real-world management. For example, ensuring that staff feel valued and supported can improve their ability to handle demanding tenants and tight deadlines.

What tensions exist between theory and real-world management?

A key tension lies in balancing organizational goals with individual needs. While theory emphasizes motivation and well-being, real-world constraints such as deadlines and tenant expectations often require immediate results.

Conclusion

Managing individuals requires a nuanced approach that integrates motivation theory with practical realities. This reflection has reinforced the importance of empathy, adaptability, and understanding human behaviour in achieving effective management outcomes.

Critical Reflection 3: Managing Sensemaking

Introduction

Sensemaking refers to the process through which individuals interpret and give meaning to complex or uncertain situations. This concept is strongly associated with Karl Weick, who emphasizes that organizations are constructed through shared interpretations (Weick, 1995).

What interested or inspired you about this topic?

This topic intrigued me because of its relevance in dealing with ambiguity and uncertainty in management. In property management, situations such as tenant disputes or eviction processes often involve incomplete information and emotional tension.

What new insights or understanding did you gain?

I learned that effective management is not just about making decisions but also about shaping how situations are understood. Sensemaking involves communication, framing, and interpretation.

For example, when dealing with a tenant who refused to vacate for over seven months, the situation required careful communication with legal teams, tenants, and internal stakeholders. Each party interpreted the situation differently, requiring alignment and clarity.

How did this topic challenge your assumptions or expand your perspective on management?

Previously, I assumed that facts alone drive decision-making. However, sensemaking highlights that perceptions and interpretations significantly influence outcomes.

How will you apply this knowledge in your current or future managerial practice?

I would apply sensemaking by:

  • Communicating clearly and consistently
  • Framing situations to align understanding among stakeholders
  • Encouraging dialogue to reduce misunderstandings

What connections can you make between theory and real-world management practice?

Sensemaking is evident in managing tenant expectations, particularly affluent tenants who demand immediate service. How policies are communicated can influence whether tenants perceive delays as inefficiency or as standard procedure.

What tensions exist between theory and real-world management?

A key tension is that while theory emphasizes interpretation, real-world management often requires quick decisions, leaving limited time for reflection.

Conclusion

Sensemaking is critical in navigating complexity and uncertainty, enhancing both decision-making and stakeholder management.

Critical Reflection 4: Managing Teams

Introduction

Managing teams is a central function of leadership, requiring the coordination of individuals with diverse skills, personalities, and work ethics toward shared organizational goals. Effective team management involves not only task allocation but also fostering accountability, discipline, and mutual respect. The work of Bruce Tuckman (1965), which outlines stages of team development forming, storming, norming, and performing provides a useful framework for understanding team dynamics. Reflecting on this topic has significantly shaped my understanding of leadership boundaries, authority, and the importance of balancing approachability with discipline.

What interested or inspired you about this topic?

My interest in managing teams was largely influenced by my practical experience leading a team in a demanding property management environment. While I initially embraced an open-door policy and positioned myself as an approachable and understanding manager, I began to observe unintended consequences.

Specifically, I noticed patterns of carelessness and declining discipline within the team. Employees began extending their break times, arriving late for duty, and struggling to prioritize urgent and important tasks. Additionally, there were instances where team members communicated in ways that reflected a lack of respect, both in tone and content.

These experiences highlighted the complexity of team management and raised critical questions about the balance between being supportive and maintaining authority. This tension sparked my interest in understanding how management theory can guide effective team leadership.

What new insights or understanding did you gain?

Through engaging with team management theory, I gained valuable insights into the importance of structure, boundaries, and role clarity. Bruce Tuckman (1965) explains that teams naturally go through a "storming" phase, where conflicts, power struggles, and behavioral challenges emerge. Reflecting on my experience, I recognized that my team was operating within this phase, where norms and expectations had not yet been firmly established.

Another important insight was the role of managerial authority and boundary-setting. While contemporary management emphasizes participative leadership and open communication, it also underscores the necessity of clear expectations and accountability (Robbins & Judge, 2017). My initial approach, which prioritized openness, inadvertently created ambiguity around acceptable behavior.

Furthermore, I developed a deeper understanding of time management and prioritization within teams. The inability of team members to distinguish between urgent and important tasks reflects a lack of structured guidance and performance monitoring. This aligns with management theories that emphasize the importance of goal-setting and performance standards in driving team effectiveness.

How did this topic challenge your assumptions or expand your perspective on management?

This topic challenged my assumption that being an approachable and understanding manager would automatically lead to respect and high performance. I initially believed that fostering a friendly and open environment would encourage accountability and professionalism.

However, my experience revealed that without clear boundaries, openness can be misinterpreted as leniency. This realization was particularly evident in the way team members began to disregard time management expectations and professional communication standards.

This reflection expanded my perspective by highlighting that effective leadership requires a balance between approachability and authority. Respect is not only built through empathy but also through consistency, fairness, and the enforcement of standards.

How will you apply this knowledge in your current or future managerial practice?

In response to these insights, I have identified several strategies to improve my team management approach:

  • Establishing clear boundaries: Clearly communicating expectations regarding punctuality, break times, and professional conduct
  • Reinforcing accountability: Monitoring performance and addressing deviations promptly
  • Setting priorities: Providing guidance on distinguishing between urgent and important tasks
  • Maintaining professional communication standards: Addressing inappropriate communication and reinforcing respect

For example, in managing daily operations such as maintenance coordination and tenant queries, I will implement structured task prioritization systems to ensure that urgent issues are addressed promptly. Additionally, I will introduce regular check-ins to monitor progress and reinforce expectations.

Importantly, I will maintain my open-door policy but within clearly defined professional boundaries. This approach ensures that employees feel supported while understanding that accountability and respect are non-negotiable.

What connections can you make between theory and real-world management practice?

The relevance of team management theory is evident in my real-world experience. The challenges I encountered such as extended breaks, late reporting, and poor prioritization reflect the "storming" phase described by Bruce Tuckman (1965). Without clear norms and leadership intervention, teams can struggle to transition to higher levels of performance.

Additionally, theories of leadership and team effectiveness emphasize the importance of role clarity and performance expectations. In practice, when these elements are not clearly defined, teams are more likely to exhibit inconsistent behavior and reduced productivity.

What tensions exist between theory and real-world management?

A key tension between theory and practice lies in balancing employee empowerment with managerial control. While theory encourages participative leadership and autonomy, real-world experience demonstrates that excessive flexibility can lead to a decline in discipline and performance.

Another tension is between maintaining relationships and enforcing standards. Addressing issues such as lateness or inappropriate communication can strain relationships, particularly in teams where the manager has established a friendly and approachable image.

Furthermore, there is a tension between ideal team behavior and operational pressures. In a fast-paced environment, managers may prioritize task completion over team development, which can result in unresolved behavioral issues.

Conclusion

Reflecting on managing teams has significantly enhanced my understanding of leadership and organizational behavior. My experience demonstrated that while approachability and support are important, they must be complemented by clear boundaries, accountability, and consistent enforcement of standards.

By integrating theoretical frameworks such as Bruce Tuckman's model with practical experience, I have developed a more balanced approach to team management. Moving forward, I will focus on creating a structured yet supportive environment that promotes both performance and respect.

Ultimately, effective team management is not about choosing between authority and empathy, but about integrating both to achieve sustainable organizational success.

Critical Reflection 5: Managing Conflict

Introduction

Conflict is an inevitable aspect of organizational life, particularly in diverse workplaces where individuals bring different backgrounds, values, and communication styles. Effective conflict management is therefore a critical managerial competency. The work of Kenneth Thomas and Ralph Kilmann (1974) provides a foundational framework for understanding how individuals respond to conflict through styles such as competing, collaborating, compromising, avoiding, and accommodating. Reflecting on this topic has deepened my understanding of the relational and behavioral dimensions of management, particularly in handling interpersonal tensions within teams.

What interested or inspired you about this topic?

My interest in managing conflict stems directly from my practical experience working with individuals from diverse cultural and social backgrounds. In my team, I have observed that conflict is not always rooted in task-related issues, but often arises from differences in communication styles, personal values, and perceptions of respect.

A specific incident that captured my attention involved two team members who experienced conflict due to one individual's communication approach. One employee expressed frustration that their colleague was overly directive, often "ordering" others around, especially in the presence of others. This created feelings of disrespect and tension within the team. This situation highlighted how interpersonal dynamics, rather than technical competence, can significantly affect team cohesion and performance.

What new insights or understanding did you gain?

Through engaging with conflict management theory, I gained valuable insights into the complexity of workplace conflict. The Kenneth Thomas and Ralph Kilmann model emphasizes that conflict is not inherently negative; rather, its outcomes depend on how it is managed (Thomas & Kilmann, 1974).

One key insight is that conflict often reflects underlying needs and perceptions, particularly around respect, recognition, and communication. In the situation I encountered, the issue was not merely about task delegation but about how instructions were communicated and perceived. This aligns with the idea that interpersonal conflict is frequently driven by emotional and relational factors rather than purely operational concerns (De Dreu & Weingart, 2003).

Additionally, I developed a stronger understanding of the importance of emotional intelligence in conflict management. Recognizing emotions, both my own and those of others, is essential in facilitating constructive dialogue and resolution (Goleman, 1998).

How did this topic challenge your assumptions or expand your perspective on management?

This topic challenged my initial assumption that conflict should be minimized or avoided in the workplace. Previously, I viewed conflict as disruptive and something that reflects poor team functioning. However, this reflection helped me realize that conflict can be a productive force when managed effectively.

In the situation between my team members, I initially considered addressing the issue informally or encouraging avoidance to maintain harmony. However, this approach would likely have allowed resentment to build over time. Instead, I recognized the importance of addressing the issue directly and constructively.

This shift in perspective has expanded my understanding of management from maintaining order to facilitating open communication and mutual understanding.

How will you apply this knowledge in your current or future managerial practice?

In response to the conflict, I arranged a formal meeting between the two team members. During the discussion, I created a safe and respectful environment for both individuals to express their concerns. I addressed the issue of communication style, emphasizing the importance of respect and professionalism in all interactions.

This approach reflects a collaborative conflict management style, which seeks to address the concerns of all parties and achieve a mutually beneficial outcome (Thomas & Kilmann, 1974). By facilitating dialogue, I was able to help both individuals understand each other's perspectives and agree on more respectful ways of communicating.

Going forward, I will apply the following strategies in my managerial practice:

  • Promoting respectful communication norms within the team
  • Addressing conflict early before it escalates
  • Encouraging open dialogue and active listening
  • Adapting conflict management styles based on the situation

For example, in high-pressure environments such as managing maintenance deadlines or dealing with demanding tenants, tensions may arise more frequently. Being proactive in setting expectations and fostering a culture of respect can help prevent conflicts from escalating.

What connections can you make between theory and real-world management practice?

Theories of conflict management are highly applicable to real-world scenarios. The incident within my team illustrates how the Thomas-Kilmann model can be applied in practice. While one individual adopted a more competing or directive style, the other responded with frustration, leading to interpersonal conflict.

By intervening and facilitating a structured discussion, I shifted the interaction toward a collaborative approach, aligning with theoretical recommendations for effective conflict resolution. This demonstrates how theory can guide practical decision-making and improve outcomes in real organizational contexts.

What tensions exist between theory and real-world management?

Despite its usefulness, there are notable tensions between conflict management theory and real-world practice. One key tension is the time constraint in operational environments. In theory, collaborative conflict resolution requires time for discussion and reflection. However, in practice, managers often face pressure to resolve issues quickly, particularly in fast-paced environments like property management.

Another tension lies in power dynamics. While theory assumes that individuals can engage in open and equal dialogue, hierarchical structures and personality differences can influence how conflict is expressed and resolved. For example, some employees may feel uncomfortable voicing concerns openly, requiring managers to be more proactive in facilitating communication.

Additionally, there is a tension between maintaining organizational authority and fostering employee autonomy. As a manager, I must ensure that policies and standards are upheld while also allowing employees to express themselves and resolve conflicts constructively.

Conclusion

Reflecting on managing conflict has significantly enhanced my understanding of the interpersonal dimensions of management. Conflict is not merely a challenge to be avoided but an opportunity for growth, improved communication, and stronger team relationships.

The experience within my team demonstrated the importance of addressing conflict directly, promoting respect, and facilitating open dialogue. By integrating theoretical frameworks such as the Thomas-Kilmann model with practical experience, I have developed a more balanced and effective approach to conflict management.

In my future managerial practice, I will continue to view conflict as a natural and potentially constructive aspect of organizational life, approaching it with empathy, structure, and a commitment to positive outcomes.
